# Runbook — Pitch Drafting

_How Mercury writes outreach that is specific, respectful, and earns attention._

---

## Purpose

Mercury's pitch drafts are the bridge between internal research and external contact. A good pitch makes the recipient feel seen — not sold to. The business should feel that we found something real about them, that we understand their market, and that we have something genuinely worth seeing.

A bad pitch reads like every other cold email. It names the business but says nothing specific about them. It makes generic claims about "modern websites" and "mobile-first design." It could have been sent to any business in any category.

This runbook ensures every pitch draft is specific, evidenced, and respects the recipient.

---

## Before You Draft

**Required inputs — all must be present:**
- `BUSINESS.md` — complete research
- `WEBSITE_AUDIT.md` OR `NO_SITE_BRIEF.md` — complete audit/brief
- `CONCEPT_BRIEF.md` — Forge's approved concept direction
- Lead score ≥ 56 (PITCH-WORTHY or HIGH PRIORITY)

**If the concept is weak or generic:** Return to Forge before drafting. A weak concept will produce a weak pitch — fixing the pitch won't fix the problem.

## The Pitch Framework

Every outreach message should follow this structure:

### 1. Opening Hook (Most Important)
The hook is everything. A bad opener means the email never gets read.

**What works:**
- A specific, observable fact about their business or their market
- A question that reframes their current situation
- An observation about their Google Business Profile (reviews, activity)
- A brief market context that makes our finding feel relevant

**What does NOT work:**
- Generic compliments ("Your business looks great!")
- Claims we can't evidence ("We were impressed by your website")
- "I came across your business" — too passive and generic
- Any variation of "Hi, we help businesses with websites"

**Hook principles:**
- Reference something REAL — something we actually observed during research
- Make it about THEM, not about us
- Create a slight cognitive tension — make them curious, not comfortable
- Never be insulting about their current state

**Good hooks examples:**

> "Dublin Elite Plumbing has 47 Google reviews and a 4.8 rating — that's the kind of reputation most businesses would kill for. But if someone searches for you online, they land on a Google Maps listing and a Facebook page. For a service that people need to trust before they call, that's a gap worth looking at."

> "When I looked up [Clinic Name] on Google, the first thing I noticed wasn't the services — it was the reviews. 38 five-star reviews, and they all say the same thing: 'Dr. [Name] explains everything clearly.' That's rare. It's also completely absent from the practice's online presence."

> "[Restaurant Name] comes up well on Google Maps — good photos, correct hours, the address is right. But when it comes to showing what actually makes the place worth seeking out, there's nothing a potential new customer can find. The website gap here is real."

### 2. Why It Matters (1–2 Sentences)

After the hook, briefly connect the observation to why it matters in their specific market.

This isn't about web design — it's about what they're losing or leaving on the table.

**Format:**
> "In [their category], where customers [their customer behaviour], the difference between a business that's findable and credible online and one that isn't, is [specific outcome]."

### 3. What We Found / What We See

This is where the concept lives — but presented as an observation, not a sales pitch.

Describe the opportunity without claiming we've already built it or that we're experts in their specific field.

**What to do:**
- Describe what a website COULD do for their specific situation
- Reference specific trust gaps or conversion gaps we observed
- Make it concrete — not "a professional website" but "a site that shows their work, explains their approach, and makes it easy to call or book without friction"

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't use the word "website" repeatedly without adding specificity
- Don't promise specific results ("double your inquiries") without basis
- Don't pitch tier structures, pricing, or service packages
- Don't compare ourselves to other web design services
- Don't claim expertise in their industry we don't have

### 4. The Offer (Optional)

A brief, low-commitment offer to show what we've put together.

**Format:**
> "If you're curious what this could look like for [Business Name], we'd be happy to share the concept direction we've put together — no commitment, no sales call required. Would you be open to a 10-minute look?"

**Key elements:**
- Low-pressure — "no commitment, no sales call"
- Specific — "concept direction" not "our services"
- Easy yes — "would you be open to" is easier to say yes to than "book a call now"
- Time-boxed — "10-minute look" sounds doable

### 5. Signature

**Name:** Use a real name — Gbemi Akadiri or similar
**Business:** Verdantia Ltd
**Contact:** Phone only if it's appropriate for the business type. No LinkedIn unless it's B2B.
**LinkedIn:** Only if the outreach is B2B context. Don't include for consumer-facing businesses.

## Channel-Specific Rules

### Email
- **Target length:** 150–220 words
- **Subject line:** Specific, not clickbait. Reference the business name or a specific observation. Not "Website opportunity" or "Can we help?"
- **Format:** Plain text, not HTML. Human, not corporate.
- **No attachments** unless specifically relevant (e.g., sharing a concept document as a PDF — requires Nero approval)

### LinkedIn
- **Connection request note:** 300 characters max. Hook only — no pitch yet. "Hi [Name], I came across [Business] while researching [category] in [area] — your approach to [specific thing] stood out. I'd like to connect."
- **Follow-up message (after accepted):** Same structure as email pitch but shorter (~150 words). Link to the concept if appropriate.
- **Do NOT pitch in the connection request.**

---

## Subject Line Guidelines

| Type | Example | When to use |
|---|---|---|
| Specific observation | "[Business Name] — a quick observation" | When the hook is strong and specific |
| Question framing | "A question about [Business Name]'s online presence" | When you want to create curiosity |
| Market angle | "What I noticed looking at [category] businesses in [area]" | When market context is the hook |
| Soft/direct | "[Business Name] and your web presence" | When nothing else fits — last resort |

**Never do:**
- "Hi" or "Introduction"
- "Partnership opportunity" (overused and vague)
- "I have an idea for your website" (too presumptuous)
- Exclamation marks (spam signal)
- ALL CAPS
- Numbers like "🚀" or "URGENT"

## Quality Checklist

Before submitting for Nero approval:

- [ ] Opening hook is specific and evidenced — not generic
- [ ] Hook references something real from our research
- [ ] The "why it matters" section is specific to their market
- [ ] The concept is described in business terms, not design jargon
- [ ] No vague claims ("modern, mobile-first, SEO-optimised")
- [ ] No insults about their current website or business
- [ ] No pressure or fake urgency
- [ ] CTA is single and low-friction
- [ ] Subject line is specific and not clickbaity
- [ ] Email length is appropriate (150–220 words)
- [ ] Nothing would be embarrassing if forwarded
- [ ] Signature is accurate
- [ ] If I received this email, would I read past the subject line?

## What to Do If the Concept Is Weak

**Do not try to write around a weak concept.** The pitch will sound hollow and generic.

Instead:
1. Flag to Nero that the concept is weak
2. Identify specifically what's missing
3. Return to Forge with specific questions
4. If the concept genuinely cannot be made strong, recommend not pitching this lead

**A genuinely thoughtful email about why their situation is interesting is better than a forced pitch about a website they don't need.**

---

## Handling Responses

### If they reply "Not interested"
Reply briefly and graciously:
> "Totally understand — no problem at all. Thanks for your time. Best of luck with the business."

Then suppress immediately. Log in STATUS.md and SUPPRESSION.md.

### If they reply "Remove me"
Reply once immediately:
> "Understood — apologies for reaching out. You've been removed from our list. Best of luck."

Suppress immediately. No further contact under any circumstances.

### If they reply with questions
Route to Nero immediately. Do not answer detailed questions about pricing, timelines, or process without Nero's involvement.

### If they reply positively
Route to Nero immediately. This is now a warm lead and requires human handling.
