# Runbook — Concept Brief

_How Forge translates Scout's research into a custom website concept direction._

---

## Purpose

The concept brief is Forge's primary deliverable. It translates raw research — business type, market position, customer journey, trust gaps, content reality — into a concrete, specific website design direction.

This is where generic web design pitches end and custom thinking begins. A good concept brief reads like someone who deeply understands this business and has a specific vision for their web presence. A bad concept brief reads like a template with the business name filled in.

---

## Before You Start

**Required inputs — all must be present before you begin:**
- `BUSINESS.md` — complete research from Scout
- `WEBSITE_AUDIT.md` OR `NO_SITE_BRIEF.md` — complete audit/brief from Scout
- Lead score ≥ 41
- Business legitimacy confirmed
- At least 2 competitor websites reviewed (in Scout's research)

**If any of these are missing or thin:** Return to Scout with specific questions. Do not fill gaps with assumptions.

---

## Your Frame

You are not designing a website. You are designing a business tool.

Every element of the concept must answer: **"Does this help this specific business acquire, convert, or retain customers?"**

If it doesn't serve the business goal, it doesn't go in the concept.

---

## Step 1 — Business Essence (First, Always)

Before writing a single design direction, answer:

- What does this business do? (Specific service, specific customer, specific geography)
- Who are their customers? (Not just "Dublin homeowners" — what's their profile, what's their need, what's their research behaviour?)
- What is the ONE thing that makes them different from the average business in this category? (If you can't answer this, go back to BUSINESS.md — you may need more research)
- What is the business trying to accomplish with new customers? (Get found? Build trust? Get them to call? Get them to book?)

This is your design compass. Everything flows from these answers.

---

## Step 2 — The Core Website Problem

Articulate the single most important problem the website needs to solve.

Not a design problem. A BUSINESS problem.

**Format:**
> "When a new customer in [location] searches for [service], they find [X competitors]. [Business Name] currently [what they have / don't have]. As a result, [specific business consequence]."

**Example:**
> "When a new patient searches for a physiotherapist in South Dublin, they find 8 clinics with professional websites showing their approach, team, and booking systems. [Clinic Name] has an active Google listing and 5-star reviews but no website — meaning every new patient must call before they've built any trust or understood the clinic's approach. As a result, [Clinic Name] loses inquiries who choose a competitor with a site they can research first."

**This is the concept's job.** Everything in your design language, layout, and structure should directly address this problem.

---

## Step 3 — Design Language

### Colour Palette

**Do not say:** "Blue and white for trust."

**Instead:** "A deep slate blue as the primary — not generic corporate blue, but a slightly warmer, more established slate. Paired with a warm cream/off-white that feels human and approachable, not clinical. Gold accent used sparingly — for CTAs and trust highlights only. This palette signals: established, expert, human, premium-but-not-cold."

**Your colour direction must:**
- Match the business's actual market position (not aspirational)
- Differentiate from their current site (if they have one)
- Differentiate from competitor websites (if reviewed)
- Work for the specific customer type (warm and approachable for wellness? Authoritative and serious for legal?)

**Palette must include:**
- Background/base
- Primary colour
- Accent/CTA colour
- For each: mood keyword + specific direction (not just a hex code)

### Typography

**Do not say:** "Modern sans-serif."

**Instead:** "Headings in a confident serif — something like DM Serif Display or Playfair Display. Not formal or stuffy, but authoritative. Body in a clean, readable humanist sans — Instrument Sans or Source Sans Pro. Never Inter (it's everywhere and signals generic). This pairing: expertise + warmth."

**Your typography direction must:**
- Match the emotional tone of the business (serious medical ≠ playful cafe)
- Work across mobile and desktop
- Not be the default Google Font that every template uses
- Have a rationale tied to the customer type and business goal

### Visual Tone

**3–4 adjectives that describe how this website should feel.** Not "modern and professional." Every website claims that. Instead: "Competent, calm, evidence-focused — like a practitioner who explains everything clearly and doesn't oversell."

**Then:** For each adjective, note what design choices create that tone (colour, typography, imagery, layout, copy weight).

### Imagery Direction

**Real or stock? Professional or casual? What mood? What subject? What is absolutely NOT appropriate?**

Examples:
- "Real photography only. The clinic's actual treatment rooms, the team's genuine smiles. Not stock photos of generic therapy settings. If professional photography isn't available at launch, we use a restrained stock approach — never generic 'smiling professional with clipboard' imagery."
- "Atmospheric food photography — this is a restaurant worth travelling for. Not phone-camera menu shots. If we can't get a photographer, the concept uses hero imagery of the space and team, not food — because food photography without professional execution undermines the positioning."

---

## Step 4 — Layout & Structure

### Homepage Priority Order

**The homepage must show things in the right order for this business and this customer.**

Not every homepage should lead with "hero image + headline." For some businesses, the hero should be social proof. For others, the service overview. For others, trust credentials.

**For each section on the homepage, state:**
1. What it is (section name)
2. What it contains (content type)
3. Why it is in this position specifically (rationale tied to the customer journey and the business goal)
4. What the mobile version looks like (first, what collapses, what stays)

**Maximum 5 sections on the homepage.** If you're tempted to add a sixth, question it hard. Homepage concepts with 8 sections are unfocused.

### Trust Architecture

**Where does trust live on this homepage?**

For each trust signal you recommend, state:
- What it is (testimonial, certification badge, years in business, Google reviews embed)
- Where it lives on the page
- Why that placement is optimal for this business

**Example:**
> "Google Reviews widget, prominently placed below the fold — after the service overview establishes what they do, the reviews confirm how well they do it. This sequence mirrors the customer's actual research process: 'What do they do? → Are they actually good at it?'"

### CTA Model

**What is the ONE primary action?**
- Phone call? → Where is the phone number? Is it a click-to-call at all times on mobile?
- Contact form? → What's on the form? How many fields? Where does it live?
- Booking widget? → Is it embedded or a redirect? How prominent?
- WhatsApp? → Is this appropriate for this customer type?

**The user journey to CTA:**
- Arrive at homepage → What do they see first? → What guides their eye? → Where is the CTA? → How many seconds to action?

**Secondary CTA:** Is there one? What? When does it appear?

---

## Step 5 — Content Requirements

**What does the client need to supply?** Be specific.

Not "photos" — "minimum 8 high-resolution images of completed work, including [specific types]."

Not "testimonials" — "minimum 3 written customer testimonials of at least 2 sentences each, speaking to [specific service or outcome]."

**What's missing that they need to develop?** Flag this. A website concept that requires content the business can't produce is an incomplete concept.

**Content gaps that are the client's problem vs. the designer's problem:**
- Client problem: Getting testimonials written, professional photography arranged, proper service descriptions written
- Designer problem: How to architect that content, how to make it load fast, how to write placeholder copy that's directionally correct

---

## Step 6 — Differentiation Rationale

This is the section that proves the concept is custom.

**How does this concept differ from:**
- Their current site (if they have one) — specific changes, not just "modern vs old"
- The competitor websites reviewed — what does this concept do that theirs don't?
- Generic templates in this category — what does this NOT look like?

**The single most differentiating element:**
One sentence on the one thing that makes this concept feel uniquely right for this business.

If you can't write this sentence, the concept isn't specific enough yet.

---

## Step 7 — Mobile-First Specifics

Mobile is not desktop with smaller screens. Address it specifically.

**For this concept:**
- What does the mobile viewport show first? (At 375px width, no scroll)
- What's the tap hierarchy? (In priority order)
- What's collapsed that was visible on desktop?
- What is always visible regardless of scroll position?
- Is the primary CTA always one tap away?

---

## Output

**Complete `CONCEPT_BRIEF.md`** from the template.

**Self-review before submitting:**
- Does this concept feel specific to this business, or would it work for any business in this category?
- Could I describe the design direction in 2 sentences without using the words "modern," "professional," "clean," or "mobile-friendly"?
- Is the CTA model genuinely clear, or is it vague?
- Is there a single differentiating element I can point to?
- Would Mercury have enough to write a pitch that's specific to this business, or would they be filling in blanks?

---

## If Research Is Thin

If BUSINESS.md or the audit/brief doesn't give you enough to produce a specific concept:

**Do not improvise.** Return to Scout with specific questions.

Common gaps:
- "What is the specific customer type?" (age, motivation, decision process)
- "What does their review content actually say?" (real quotes, not just rating)
- "What is the competitor landscape specifically?"
- "What is the ONE thing that makes this business different?"

Improvising a concept from thin research will produce generic output. Better to pause and get the research right.
