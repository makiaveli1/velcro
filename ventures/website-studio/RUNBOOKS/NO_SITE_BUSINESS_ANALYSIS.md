# Runbook — No-Site Business Analysis

_How Scout builds a compelling brief when a business has no website._

---

## Purpose

A no-site business is not a non-business. Many legitimate, successful businesses operate without a proper website — sometimes by choice, sometimes by neglect, sometimes because they don't yet know what they're missing.

The no-site brief must demonstrate that we understand the business deeply AND that we understand what their digital absence is costing them. We must not manufacture urgency or imply the business is failing — but we must be honest and specific about the opportunity gap.

**This is often the highest-opportunity lead type.** No existing site means no redesign friction — we can start fresh with a clean concept.

---

## Before You Start

**Confirm this is actually a no-site case:**
- No website found via Google search (not just a weak site — genuinely no site)
- If they have a Facebook/Instagram page as their primary web presence, they are a social-only case — treat differently (still a no-site brief but note the existing presence)
- If they have a directory listing with basic info, that's not a website — still qualifies as no-site

**Confirm the business is legitimate:**
- Google Business Profile with real information
- Active social media with genuine engagement (not just a profile with zero posts)
- Consistent name/address/phone across sources
- Evidence of actual operations (reviews, photos, posts)

If you can't verify basic legitimacy, this is not a qualified lead — suppress and move on.

---

## Step 1 — Map the Current Digital Presence

**Document everything that exists about this business online:**

**Google Business Profile:**
- Business name, address, phone (NAP consistency)
- Categories selected
- Hours (if posted)
- Reviews: how many, what rating, what are the reviews saying?
- Photos: have they posted photos? What do they show?
- Posts: any Google Posts? How active?
- Q&A: any questions answered?

**Social media:**
- Which platforms? (Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, TikTok, etc.)
- How active? (Last post date, posting frequency)
- What do they post? (Service photos? Educational content? Personal posts?)
- Follower count (rough)
- Engagement level (likes, comments, shares — are they real engagement or very low?)

**Directories:**
- TrueROI, Golden Pages, Yelp, Houzz, TripAdvisor, industry-specific directories
- What information is listed?
- Is it accurate and current?
- Are there reviews on these platforms?

**Review sites:**
- Google Reviews (already covered in GBP)
- Any other review platforms? (TripAdvisor, Houzz, Booking.com, etc.)
- What are reviewers saying?

---

## Step 2 — The Customer Research Journey

**Simulate the journey of a potential new customer trying to research this business.**

Use a private/incognito browser — no cookies or personalization.

**Scenario:** A person in {location} who needs {service type} hears about this business. They Google it. What do they find?

**Document what a researcher finds:**
- Google Knowledge Panel (if one exists)
- Google Maps listing
- Any social media profiles
- Any directory listings
- Any news or blog mentions

**Then ask:**
- Is it easy to find their contact information?
- Can I understand what they do and who they serve?
- Can I get a sense of their quality and reputation?
- Is there anything that helps me trust them before I've even contacted them?
- What questions do I still have that I can't answer online?

**The answers to these questions become the gap analysis.**

---

## Step 3 — What the Reviews Tell Us

**Review content is gold for no-site businesses.** Even without a website, customers are forming opinions — and writing them down.

**Extract patterns from reviews:**
- What do happy customers consistently praise? (This is their strength — it should be on their website)
- What do customers consistently mention as pain points or gaps? (This is intelligence for the concept)
- What do customers wish they had known before using the service? (This is content opportunity)
- Are there patterns that suggest a specific type of customer? (Families? Professionals? Budget-conscious? Premium clients?)

**Example:**
> Review: "We've been using Dublin Elite Plumbing for 3 years now. They're always on time, explain what they're doing, and leave the place spotless. Wish they'd had a proper website — I always have to dig through Facebook to find their number."
>
> **What this tells us:** Customers are actively looking for them online. Their Facebook page is their de facto website. They have a loyalty problem disguised as a convenience problem.

---

## Step 4 — Market Context Analysis

**What does the market look like?**

For the business's category and location:
- What is the typical online presence of a comparable competitor?
- What does a well-run competitor's website look like (if they have one)?
- What's the Google Search result page like when searching for this service in this area?
- Are there 5 competitors with websites and this business is the only one without?

**The gap is biggest when:**
- Competitors have websites and this business doesn't
- Customers are searching online for this type of service
- The business has genuine differentiation that can't be communicated without a website

**The gap is smaller when:**
- No one in this category has a particularly strong web presence
- The business relies primarily on word-of-mouth or existing client loyalty
- The customer journey for this service type doesn't involve online research

---

## Step 5 — The Opportunity Gap

**This is the most important section of the brief.**

Articulate, specifically, what this business is missing by not having a website — and what that costs them.

**Define the gap in business terms:**

{Example:}
> **The Gap:** When potential new customers in Dublin 2 search for an emergency plumber, they find 4 competitors with professional websites, Google-ads landing pages, and clear contact options. [Business Name] does not appear in those search results with any web presence. The only way a new customer can find them is if they've heard of them through word of mouth or seen their Google Maps listing.
>
> **The Cost:** Based on their GBP review volume (47 reviews) and typical conversion rates, [Business Name] is likely receiving approximately X inquiries per month from existing reputation alone. A professional website with local SEO foundations and clear trust signals could increase this by an estimated Y%, representing Z additional qualified inquiries per month.
>
> **The Timing Problem:** Every month without a website is months of lost discovery revenue from customers who would have chosen them if they'd been findable.

**Be specific. Be honest. Don't overstate.**

If the gap is real and significant, this should be easy to demonstrate with evidence from the review content, the competitor analysis, and the customer journey simulation.

---

## Step 6 — What a Website Should Do For This Business

**Based on everything above, recommend what this site should accomplish.**

**Primary goal:** (Phone calls? Form submissions? Online bookings? Directional visits?)

**Secondary goals:** (Build email list? Showcase portfolio? Educate customers?)

**Who is the primary visitor?** (New customers finding them via Google? Existing customers looking for contact info? Referral partners?)

**What does the visitor need to believe before they take action?**
- That this business is legitimate and established
- That this business can solve their specific problem
- That this business is better than alternatives
- That it's easy to contact or book

**What does the site need to communicate to make that happen?**
- Specific trust signals for this business type
- Specific content that answers the questions customers always ask
- Specific proof points from their actual customer base

---

## Step 7 — Concept Fit Check

Before submitting the brief, check:

- **Do we have enough substance to build a custom concept?** If the business has no reviews, no social media, no directory presence, and no distinguishing information — we may not have enough to produce a genuinely specific concept. Flag this honestly.

- **Can the business supply the content we'd need?** If we recommend professional photography, do they have a photographer? If we recommend testimonials, do they have any? A concept is only as good as its execution. Flag content gaps.

- **Is the timing right?** Is this a business that is actively trying to grow, or one that is comfortable with their current customer base? A website helps the former more than the latter.

**Complete `NO_SITE_BRIEF.md`** using the template.

**Key sections to be strong:**
- How customers find this business right now (be specific)
- What information is available vs. absent (the gap)

---

## Quality Bar

**The brief is complete when:**
- You can describe what a customer experiences when researching this business online — specifically
- The opportunity gap is articulated in business terms (not just "no website")
- You could write a compelling opening hook for the pitch based on this brief
- Forge has enough specificity to produce a genuinely custom concept
- The brief would help a skeptical business owner understand why a website matters for their specific situation

**The brief is NOT complete if:**
- It's a generic "they need a website because everyone needs a website" argument
- The opportunity gap is stated vaguely without business impact
- You couldn't write a specific pitch hook from it
- Forge would still be guessing about the concept direction
