# Website Studio — Design Standard

_For Forge. Every concept must meet this bar._

---

## The Standard: Custom, Not Template

The entire value proposition of this system rests on one thing: **the concepts we show are genuinely custom and specific to the business we're pitching.**

A generic template concept is worse than no concept — it signals we don't actually understand the business, and it makes the pitch feel like every other cold web design email they've received.

Every concept brief must demonstrate:
1. We understand their business
2. We understand their market
3. We understand their customer
4. We have a specific, considered vision for what their site should do and look like
5. That vision is differentiated from what they have now AND from what their competitors likely have

---

## What "Modern, Premium, Custom" Actually Means

Not "use a modern template and change the colours."

Modern, premium, custom means:
- **The design serves the business goal** — not an aesthetic exercise
- **Every element earns its place** — if it doesn't help the user act or trust, it goes
- **The typography is considered** — not just "Google Fonts default"
- **The colour palette is specific to the business** — not a generic "blue and white professional"
- **The layout has rhythm** — not just a header-hero-3-columns-footer template
- **The mobile experience is the primary design surface** — not an afterthought
- **Trust signals are architecture** — built in, not bolted on
- **Accessibility is baseline** — colour contrast, readable text, clear navigation
- **Performance is a feature** — fast loading is a trust signal
- **The brand comes through** — if we can't tell anything about the business personality from the design direction, it hasn't been thought about enough

## Concept Brief Requirements

Every `CONCEPT_BRIEF.md` must include:

### 1. Design Language Direction
- **Colour palette:** Not just hex codes — mood, warmth, what the colours communicate about the business
- **Typography:** Direction (serif for authority? clean sans for modern? warm and approachable?) — specific typeface recommendations with rationale
- **Visual tone:** Serious? Approachable? Premium? Playful? Community-focused? Clinical? — be specific, not "modern and professional"
- **Imagery direction:** What kind of photography or visual assets? Real photography vs illustration? What mood?

### 2. Layout and Structure
- **Homepage priority order:** What does the site show first, second, third — and WHY that order for this specific business
- **Key sections:** What must the homepage have (and what should it NOT have — feature creep kills concepts)
- **Trust architecture:** Where do trust signals live? Testimonials, certifications, years in business, associations — built into the structure, not a footer afterthought
- **CTA model:** What's the primary call-to-action? Phone? Form? Booking widget? — and what is the user journey to get there

### 3. Mobile-First Notes
- How does the layout adapt? What is the primary mobile experience?
- What collapses, what stays prominent?
- Is the CTA always visible?

### 4. Content Recommendations
- What does the client need to supply? (Copy, photography, certifications, testimonials)
- What's missing that they need to develop? (Logo refresh, professional photography, proper copy)
- What's the content priority if they can only do three things first?

### 5. Differentiation Notes
- How is this concept different from what they have now?
- How is it different from what their competitors likely have?
- What is the one thing that makes this concept feel uniquely right for THIS business?

## Category-Specific Concept Guidance

### Trades / Professional Services
- **Primary goal:** Phone call or contact form submission
- **Trust architecture:** licences, certifications, insurance, years in business, local reviews, before/after
- **Design tone:** Clean, confident, competent. Not flashy. Signals reliability.
- **Layout priority:** Service overview → Trust signals → Contact/CTA (immediate)
- **Mobile:** Tap-to-call must be prominent and always visible

### Wellness / Clinics
- **Primary goal:** Booking / enquiry
- **Trust architecture:** Practitioner credentials, patient testimonials, clinic atmosphere photos, safety standards, affiliations
- **Design tone:** Calm, professional, warm. Not clinical-cold. Not spa-frivolous.
- **Layout priority:** What we treat/do → Who we are → How to book
- **Mobile:** Booking widget or prominent phone — no friction to action

### Hospitality / Food
- **Primary goal:** Reservation or visit
- **Trust architecture:** Ambience photography, menu, location/hours, reviews, events
- **Design tone:** Matches the venue personality precisely — gastropub ≠ fine dining ≠ cafe
- **Layout priority:** Visual hook (hero imagery) → What/where/when → Menu/booking → Social proof
- **Mobile:** Map, hours, and booking must be immediately accessible without scrolling

### Consultants / Coaches
- **Primary goal:** Qualified enquiry / discovery call
- **Trust architecture:** Expert positioning, client testimonials, case studies/results, thought leadership content, clear specialisation
- **Design tone:** Authoritative but human. Personal brand, not corporate. Evidence of impact.
- **Layout priority:** Who you help → What you do → Proof/results → How to connect
- **Mobile:** Clear pathway to enquiry — no dead ends

## What Bad Concepts Look Like

**These will be rejected and sent back to Forge for revision:**

1. **Template descriptions** — "A modern, clean, professional website with a hero section and three service columns" — this describes 40% of all websites
2. **No differentiation rationale** — "This will make them stand out" without explaining HOW
3. **Design mismatch** — A sleek, dark, minimalist concept for a warm, community-focused physiotherapy clinic
4. **Generic trust signals** — "We'll add testimonials somewhere" without explaining placement, type, and architecture
5. **No CTA clarity** — Multiple competing calls-to-action, or a CTA that requires too many steps
6. **Ignoring the existing brand** — A complete visual overhaul that throws away any existing brand equity without rationale
7. **Too many sections** — Homepage concepts with 8+ sections — the priority order gets lost
8. **No mobile consideration** — "On desktop, this will look great" — not acceptable
9. **Generic colour palettes** — "Blue and white for trust" — every bank and software company has this palette
10. **Copy as an afterthought** — "We'll write compelling copy" without any direction on what that copy should say

## Visual Reference Standards

If referencing existing websites as style direction:
- Use real URLs, not "think about X website"
- Name the specific elements that work (typography, layout, trust signal placement)
- Explain why that element works for THIS business concept
- Do not reference competitor websites directly — use aspirational references from unrelated industries if needed to avoid direct copying

## Approval Gate for Concepts

Forge's concept brief goes to Mercury and Nero for review before it becomes the basis for a pitch.

Review questions:
- Does this concept feel specific to this business?
- Does Mercury have enough to write a genuinely tailored pitch?
- Is there a clear differentiation from their current site?
- Is the CTA model sensible and clear?
- Does the concept avoid generic template language?

If the answer to any of these is "no" or "maybe": the brief goes back to Forge with specific notes.

## Accessibility Baseline

Every concept must meet these minimum standards:
- Colour contrast ratio ≥ 4.5:1 for normal text, ≥ 3:1 for large text (WCAG AA)
- Font size minimum 16px for body text
- All interactive elements must be keyboard accessible
- Images must have alt text (this is a content requirement for the client)
- No auto-playing media
- Form labels must be visible and associated with inputs
- Page must have a logical reading order independent of visual layout

These are not optional — they are the minimum standard for a site we would be proud to associate with.
